Google Empowerment Tools

Interactive digital tools for creative self-expression and identity exploration.

MusicFX DJ

Create music beats that represent your story

Google Tool

Use this tool to create a short beat that represents your background, then share your "Where I'm From" story over it.

Open MusicFX DJ Tool

How to Use (Student Perspective):

  1. Click the link above to open the MusicFX DJ tool
  2. Create a short beat that feels like your background or where you're from
  3. Think about your "Where I'm From" story while the beat plays
  4. When it's your turn, play your beat and share your story over it
  5. Listen for common threads in other students' stories

Example: "Where I'm from, most kids don't know how to read. But when I was 7, my mom would sit me down and make me read books after dinner."

Whisk (Art Creation)

Use art to reflect on identity

Google Tool

Create a digital self-portrait or "Picasso" that represents your identity when words aren't enough.

Open Whisk Tool

How to Use (Student Perspective):

  1. Open the Whisk tool using the link above
  2. Think: "If you could make a painting to introduce yourself, what would it be?"
  3. Create a drawing or design that represents your identity
  4. Be prepared to explain your choices to the group
  5. Try to match other students' drawings to the artists during the activity

Tip: Your drawing can be abstract - it doesn't have to look like you. It should represent who you are inside.

Doppl (Style & Outfits)

Connect style to confidence and leadership

Google Tool

Design an outfit for a professional setting and explain how it reflects who you are.

Open Doppl Tool

How to Use (Student Perspective):

  1. Open the Doppl tool using the link above
  2. Break into small groups if instructed
  3. Design or describe an outfit for: job interview, networking event, or formal occasion
  4. Present your outfit to the group and explain how it reflects your identity
  5. Discuss why presentation matters in leadership

Remember: At Morehouse, presentation matters. How you present yourself connects to confidence and how people see you.

Workshop Activities & Games

Interactive games and activities for building brotherhood, exploring identity, and creating impact.

Section 2: Identity Activities

Explore who you are through creative expression

Activity 1: Where I'm From (with MusicFX DJ)

What you'll do: Create a short beat that represents your background, then share your "Where I'm From" story over it.

Student Instructions:

  1. Listen to the facilitator's example first
  2. Create your beat using the MusicFX DJ tool
  3. Start your story with "Where I'm from..." and share something meaningful
  4. Play your beat and share your story when it's your turn
  5. Listen actively to others' stories - look for commonalities

Activity 2: Who I'm Not

What you'll do: Flip negative self-statements into positive affirmations.

Student Instructions:

  1. Think of a negative thing you sometimes say about yourself
  2. Flip it into a positive statement (Example: "I'm not weak → I am strong")
  3. Share your positive statement with the group when ready
  4. The group will celebrate each positive statement
  5. Really try to believe the positive statement about yourself

Activity 3: Pick Your Picasso (with Whisk)

What you'll do: Create an art piece that represents your identity.

Student Instructions:

  1. Get an index card and pen/pencil if not using the digital tool
  2. Draw a tattoo you would get or a self-portrait that represents you
  3. You have 2-3 minutes to create your "Picasso"
  4. All cards will be collected and redistributed
  5. Try to match the drawings to the artists in the room
  6. Explain your drawing when it's returned to you

Section 3: Brotherhood Activities

Build trust through vulnerability and shared support

Activity 1: Fear For Me

What you'll do: Share a fear and receive support from others.

Student Instructions:

  1. Think of one fear you're comfortable sharing
  2. When it's your turn, share your fear with the group
  3. The next person will respond with support, encouragement, or advice
  4. Listen carefully when others share - offer genuine support
  5. Remember: What's said here, stays here

Activity 2: If You Knew Me

What you'll do: Complete the sentence "If you really knew me, you'd know..."

Student Instructions:

  1. Listen to the facilitator's example first
  2. Complete the sentence: "If you really knew me, you'd know..."
  3. Share something meaningful about yourself
  4. Go around the circle - you can pass if you need to
  5. Push yourself to share something deeper if you're comfortable

Activity 3: The Line Game

What you'll do: Step forward if a statement applies to you.

Student Instructions:

  1. Stand on one side of the tape line on the floor
  2. Listen carefully to each question read by the facilitator
  3. If the statement applies to you, step forward over the line
  4. Look around to see who else steps forward
  5. Questions will range from light to deeper
  6. You don't have to explain why you stepped forward

Example Questions: "Do you own a pair of Jordans?" "Do you live with both parents?" "Do you know someone in a gang?"

Activity 4: A Warm Wind Blows

What you'll do: A movement game that highlights shared experiences.

Student Instructions:

  1. Form a big circle with enough space to move
  2. Listen for the phrase: "A warm wind blows for anyone who..."
  3. If the description applies to you, move to a different spot in the circle
  4. The last person to find a spot goes to the middle and says the next phrase
  5. Be honest about whether the trait applies to you
  6. Move quickly but safely to find a new spot

Section 4: Action & Impact Activities

Connect personal growth to social change

Activity 1: Spotlight on Change Agents

What you'll do: Research and discuss someone making a difference.

Student Instructions:

  1. Research or think about someone making a difference (local, national, or global)
  2. Prepare to present this person to the group
  3. Share: Who they are, what they do, why they inspire you
  4. Listen to others' presentations
  5. Discuss what makes an effective change agent

Activity 2: Protecting Our Future (Letter Writing)

What you'll do: Write letters supporting funding for HBCUs and minority-serving institutions.

Student Instructions:

  1. Get paper and pen
  2. Write a letter to a representative supporting funding for HBCUs, HSIs, TCUs, and MSIs
  3. Explain why education equity matters to you
  4. Share your letter with the group if comfortable
  5. Discuss why we should care about schools beyond our own

Core Values & Workshop Principles

The foundational principles that guide the Empowerment Workshop experience.

Brotherhood

Have each other's backs. Build trust through vulnerability and shared support. Brotherhood means I've got your back, and you've got mine.

Accountability

Own your actions. Take responsibility for your growth and impact. Be honest about your strengths and areas for improvement.

Growth

Leave better than you came. Commit to continuous personal development. Embrace challenges as opportunities to learn.

Guidance Over Lecture

Your job isn't to "lecture." Your job is to guide the room and keep the energy real. Facilitators are here to support, not dictate.

Respect the Space

Listen actively. Share honestly. What's said here, stays here. Create a safe environment for vulnerability.

Energy Matters

Keep energy high whether 10 or 100 students. Your engagement sets the tone for the entire workshop.

Setting the Tone

Why first impressions matter: They set the foundation for trust and openness in the workshop. When you introduce yourself with pride, it gives others permission to do the same.

Ice Breakers: These activities help break tension and allow everyone to see who's in the room. Don't rush through them - only move on when the room feels ready.

Facilitator Notes & FAQ

Important guidance for workshop leaders and frequently asked questions.

Essential Facilitator Guidelines

Critical Rules for Facilitators

  • Keep energy high whether there are 10 or 100 students in the room
  • If a student shares about self-harm → report it immediately to the chaperone or local authority
  • Never fake knowledge. If you don't know, say "I don't know"
  • No phone numbers. IG/email is fine for contact
  • If you need help, call your support (don't text) - see numbers below
  • You don't need to get through the entire curriculum! The flow of your classroom is up to you

Frequently Asked Questions

1. What do we do when we run out of activities?

Spend time talking to the students. Ask them how they felt about the activities they did. If they're comfortable, they'll start sharing more about their lives.

2. What if I only have 10 kids in my room?

Run through the curriculum like there are 100 kids in the room. Keep the same energy and give your all.

3. Can I take pictures?

Yes. Make sure to tag Morehouse, Bonner, SGA, etc.

4. What if I don't know how to run an activity?

Read the directions. If you have someone who has participated in GOTB before, ask them. If they don't know, call the support numbers above.

5. I have too many students, I need extra support. Who do I call?

Call the support contacts listed above.
